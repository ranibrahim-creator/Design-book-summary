01
The Framework

Every design decision must answer three questions.

Before you open your mouth in any meeting, you need written answers to these. Not in your head — written down. The act of writing uncovers motivations you didn't know you had.

1

What problem does it solve?

If your team hasn't set goals or KPIs, write them yourself. Projects without measurable goals languish because there's no way to prove anyone right. It just becomes opinions.

2

How does it affect the user?

Describe your design using only words — as if explaining on the phone. This reveals hidden motivations. "When you tap the button, it loads results the way SocialApp does" — ok but WHY does SocialApp do it? Is your context similar?

3

Why is it better than the alternative?

Don't throw away rejected ideas. Keep them. Your stakeholder WILL suggest one of those alternatives. If you've already tried it and know why it's worse, you're 10 steps ahead.

🌶️ Uncomfortable Truth

"The most articulate person usually wins — not the most talented."

Your design might be revolutionary, but an aggressive, well-spoken salesperson is more likely to get support if they can convince your boss they're right. Designers who can't explain why they did what they did end up on the losing side, forced to make changes simply because they were unable to fend them off.

02
Stakeholder Psychology

The stuff about stakeholders no one tells you.

We're great at empathizing with users. We're terrible at doing the same for the people who control whether our work ships.

🧠

Attribution bias is sabotaging you

You see their behavior as personality ("they're not a designer"). They see their own behavior as situational ("I just came from a stressful meeting"). You're probably wrong about why they're reacting the way they are. It almost never has anything to do with your design.

📢

Their #1 hidden need: reporting up

The most common driver of stakeholder reactions is this: they need to talk to OTHER people about your work. An engineering director kept asking "Where are the designs?" — not because he cared about pixels, but because he had nothing to show at his weekly leadership meeting.

🎭

Stated goals ≠ personal goals

Official mandate: "improve conversion." Manager's personal agenda: "migrate off the old platform ASAP because he's paying for both." Knowing the difference completely changed how the designer communicated priorities.

🔑

Your role: arm them, not convince them

Stop trying to get approval. Instead, give stakeholders the tools and vocabulary to represent YOUR work to THEIR bosses. If they can confidently explain your design to someone else, they'll support you as a byproduct.

👔

Executive

Brain: Context-Switching

They switched topics 5 times today. Bring them up to speed in 30 seconds or lose them.

💻

Developer

Brain: Effort-Based

They don't "see" your UI details. They see rework. Show value to justify the extra effort.

📦

Product Owner

Brain: Vision-Forward

Show both the MVP and the "no-limits vision." They need to see where this is going to support what's here now.

📋

Project Manager

Brain: Timeline-Based

Any change = scope impact. Always tell them how changes affect the schedule before they ask.

🎨

Marketing

Brain: Brand-Based

They don't think about interface controls. Help them evolve their brand guide to include buttons and form styles.

03
Meeting Engineering

The meeting tactics most people skip.

You design interfaces to reduce cognitive load. Do the same for your meetings.

🧲

Memory hack: Primacy & Recency

People only remember the first and last things you say — everything in the middle is lost. Pharma companies put medical disclaimers right at the drop-off point. Break your content into small chunks to create more "firsts" and "lasts."

🦈

Surprise aids memory

Kittens on a data slide. Fortune cookies taped under chairs. A quiz-show format where stakeholders pick categories. People remember what they didn't expect — use it to anchor your key message.

🚫

The "Once" rule for distractions

If the same distraction comes up at two meetings, that's your fault. One designer used a terrible placeholder icon for three meetings. Should've been fixed after meeting one. Remove: lorem ipsum, wrong stock images, misaligned wireframes.

🤝

Build your alliance BEFORE the room

Show your work to allies early. Ask them directly: "Will you speak up if there's disagreement?" It's extremely hard for an executive to overrule you when every other expert in the room agrees.

🌶️ Counterintuitive

"Never discuss design by email or chat. Record a 2.5-minute video instead."

Async text discussions demean your work by focusing only on visuals while allowing knee-jerk reactions without you there. A short screen-recorded video preserves your tone and presentation, forces them to wait until they have mental capacity to respond properly, and — surprisingly — stakeholders love them, share them, and ask for more.

🌶️ Tactical

"Name your calendar invites strategically."

Don't use "Design Review" or their name. Use: "Tom Greever, Case Study UX" or "Homepage Content, UX Team." Words like review, sync, feedback are unnecessary — they're implied. Include an executive summary and links in the invite body so they can prep without searching.

04
Listening Techniques

The listening moves that actually change outcomes.

⛏️

Excavate the real problem

"Move this button" = the proximity is confusing. "Too many colors, like a rainbow" = I don't know where to look. "Reorder these input fields" = the exported spreadsheet columns don't match the form (she didn't know exports could be customized independently). Ask: "What problem are you trying to solve?"

🔄

Convert "likes" to "works"

When someone says "I don't like it," you can't argue with their opinion. But when you rephrase to "What doesn't WORK about it?", they're forced to think about effectiveness instead of preference. Strike "like" from your vocabulary entirely.

⏸️

The 3-second pause

When they stop talking, don't respond. Count to three. It ensures they're done (people add important things after pausing), gives your brain catch-up time, and communicates their words matter enough to consider. On calls, say "mm" or "let me think about that" to avoid dead-air awkwardness.

📝

Always include the WHY in notes

Teams write down WHAT was decided but not WHY. Months later: "Why did we do this?" and nobody remembers. One phrase is enough: "Button color changing to red per new brand standards." Without the why, your notes are useless for avoiding repeat conversations.

05
Mindset & Language

The words that change everything.

⭐ Single Most Impactful Technique

Always lead with the word YES.

Start every single response with "yes." Not as a trick or a lie — as a reminder you're on the same team. "Yes, that's a great point." "Yes, I agree we need to solve this." "Yes, I understand where you're coming from." This gets more positive feedback from readers than any other idea in the book. The awkwardness is felt more by the deliverer than the recipient. Never follow "yes" with "but" — it's just another way of saying no. Just drop the "but" and the sentence still works.

The vocabulary swap

"You're wrong" → "Here's a different perspective"
"From a design perspective..." → "The reason we did it this way..."
"I like / don't like" → "This works / doesn't work because..."
"Yes, but..." → "Yes, [drop the but entirely]"
Jargon (CTA, modal, accordion) → The words THEY used in the meeting
🌶️ Role Reversal

"You're not there to receive feedback. You're there to facilitate a conversation."

Most designers enter meetings expecting to absorb criticism and defend work. Flip it: you are leading a discussion about solutions. Your role is to take what stakeholders give you and deliver it back better. You're not a punching bag. You're a guide.

The transition before your actual response

🙏

Thank

"Thanks for sharing your thoughts..."

🔁

Repeat

Briefly summarize what they said — proves you listened

🎬

Prepare

Foreshadow agreement: "I think you'll see why..."

06
Your Response

Strategy, tactics, and the IDEAL formula.

5 Tactical moves

1 Show a comparison — side by side, same canvas
2 Propose an alternative — take their idea, make it better
3 Give them a choice — show what they'll LOSE
4 Ask others to weigh in — call allies by name, stay neutral
5 Postpone — "Let me work on this for a few hours"
🌶️ Psychology

"Fear of loss is more powerful than promise of gain."

This is why coupons expire and early-bird rates exist. Use it: "If we add that CTA, it moves the login form below the fold." "Adding this means we drop dashboard graphs from this release." "If we put that in the menu, we need to remove either Preferences or History." Show the Rubik's Cube effect — turning one side affects all the others.

The IDEAL Response formula

Without the L, you only have an IDEA. With it, you have something IDEAL.

I

Identify the problem

State it so everyone's aligned. "Our challenge is users don't notice the results updating automatically."

D

Describe your solution

Connect design to the problem. "We added a Done button that gives a sense of completion."

E

Empathize with the user

Tell a specific story. Not "users might be confused" — "A parent in-store with their kids tapped multiple times, frustrated."

A

Appeal to the business

Connect to KPIs. "This will show products faster, improving add-to-cart rate."

L

Lock in agreement

Ask directly: "Do you agree?" Frame it so disagreeing sounds risky: "...or is it worth the risk of lower conversion?"

07
After the Meeting + Dealing with Changes

The uncomfortable truths about what happens next.

🍔

The McDonald's Theory

When nobody can decide, suggest McDonald's — suddenly everyone has an opinion. Same with design: make a decision and announce it. "If I don't hear back by end of day, I'm moving forward with this." A developer uses appalling CSS colors (hot pink, putrid brown) to guarantee designers will restyle his work.

🦆

Painting a duck

A game animator, tired of endless change requests, added a ridiculous pet duck to a character. The producer said "Looks great, just remove the duck." The rest went untouched. Disney animators in the 1940s drew body hair on characters they wanted rejected ("hairy arms"). Obviously don't deceive — but recognize that all relationships are give and take.

🗑️

Filter ruthlessly after meetings

Not everything said deserves to survive. One charismatic person went off on a wild tangent about 3D augmented reality store maps. The designer wrote notes (to show respect), then intentionally left the idea off the follow-up email. It was never mentioned again.

📊

Parkinson's Law of Triviality

A committee approving a nuclear power plant spent most of its time debating what material to use for the staff bike shed. People give disproportionate feedback on trivial visible issues because they're easy to grasp. This is "bike-shedding" — and it happens in every design meeting.

The trust bank account

Every interaction is a deposit or withdrawal. Your goal: stakeholders' default becomes assuming your choices are right instead of questioning every decision.

💰 Deposits

  • Agreeing with their suggestion (even strategically)
  • Metrics improving as you promised
  • Admitting when you're wrong quickly
  • Making them look good in their own meetings
  • Following up within the hour

💸 Withdrawals

  • Pushing back (even when you're right)
  • Missing deadlines
  • Designs shown to be ineffective
  • Making excuses instead of owning mistakes
  • Ignoring their feedback
🌶️ Uncomfortable Stat

"4% of executives may be clinical sociopaths."

Research suggests 4% of people are clinical sociopaths — and they're 4× more likely to hold corporate executive roles. They're task-driven and success-focused at the expense of empathy. This doesn't mean your CEO is dangerous, but it explains why some decisions feel disconnected from reason. However — most "unreasonable" stakeholders aren't sociopaths. They just want to be heard.

08
Hand This to Your Boss

What executives get wrong about working with designers.

⚡

"All you have to do is move this, right?"

No. Moving one element requires evaluating every other decision. There's a ripple effect. Ask about impact before insisting on changes. The simplest suggestion can break an entire user flow.

🐌

Indecision is the #1 project killer

The difference between a scrappy startup and a bureaucratic behemoth is decision speed. Waiting a few days for the next meeting means designers sit idle or rework designs to accommodate changes. Daily design reviews produce the best results — and the happiest teams.

🎯

Don't claim to be the user

Every user is different. You don't represent the target market any more than the designer does. "I was showing it to my spouse because they're part of our target audience" is not usability testing.

💺

Put a designer at the executive table

Companies that struggle with design don't have a talent problem — they have a leadership problem. Everyone says they want to be like Apple, but few are willing to put a designer in a VP role. Better communication helps, but it's not the main problem.

"Your ability to be thoughtful about a problem and articulate any solution is more important than your ability to design the perfect solution every time."

— Tom Greever
